Exercise 2.3-3 from chapter 2 - Getting started

Use mathematical induction to show that when \(n\) is an exact power of 2, the solution of the recurrence $$ T(n)=\begin{cases} 2, & \text{if \(n = 2 \)}.\\ 2T(n/2) + n, & \text{if \(n = 2^k\), for \(k > 1\)}. \end{cases} $$ is \(T(n) = n\,\)lg\(\;n\).

Note that the recurrence is only defined for power numbers that are powers of two. That is, for the term \(kth\), \(S(k) = T(2^k)\). Let's see if the hypotesis is viable, let's start with the second term in the series: \begin{align} T(4) & = 2T\left(\frac{4}{2}\right) + 4 \\ & = 2T(2) + 4 \\ & = 4 + 4 \\ & = 8 \end{align} Next, we check if the hypothesis holds: \begin{align} T(4) & = 4\,lg(4) \\ & = 4(2) \\ & = 8 \end{align} Now, for the next term \(n = 8\) \begin{align} T(8) & = 2T\left(\frac{8}{2}\right) + 8 \\ & = 2T(4) + 8 \\ & = 16 + 8 \\ & = 24 \\ \end{align} \begin{align} T(8) & = 8\,lg(8) \\ & = 8(3) \\ & = 24 \end{align} The hypothesis is viable. Next we begin with the base case \(1\) \begin{align} S(1) = T(2^1) = 2 & = 2^1\,lg\,2^1 \\ & = 2(1) \\ & = 2 \end{align} Now, if \(S(k) = T(2^k) = 2^k\,lg\,2^k\), then: \begin{align} S(k+1) = T(2^{k+1}) &= 2^{k+1} + 2T\left(\frac{2^{k+1}}{2}\right) \\ & = 2^{k+1} + 2T(2^k) \\ & = 2^{k+1} + 2(2^k\,lg\,2^k) \\ & = 2^{k+1} + 2^{k+1}\,lg\,2^{k} \\ & = (1 + lg2^k)(2^{k+1}) \\ & = (lg2 + lg2^k)(2^{k+1}) \\ & =(2^{k+1})(lg2^{k+1}) \\ \end{align} Then \(T(n) = nlgn\)
Q.E.D
