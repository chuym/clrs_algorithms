Exercises 7.4

  1. Show that the expression $q^2 + (n - q - 1)^2$ achieves a maximum over $q = 0,1...,n-1$ when $q = 0$ or $q = n - 1$

    $$ \begin{align} f(q) &= q^2 + (n - q - 1)^2 \\ f'(q) &= 2q + 2 \cdot (n - q - 1) \cdot [n - q - 1]' \\ f'(q) &= 2q + 2 \cdot (n - q - 1) \cdot (0 - 1 - 0) \\ f'(q) &= 2q - 2 \cdot (n - q - 1) \\ f'(q) &= 2q - 2n + 2q + 2 \\ f'(q) &= 4q - 2n + 2 \\ f'(q) &= 4q - 2n + 2 && \text{This is the first derivative of the expression} \\ f''(q) &= 4 && \text{This is the second derivative of the expression} \\ \end{align} $$ The critical point of the derivative is reached when $f'(q) = 0$: $$ \begin{align} 4q - 2n + 2 &= 0 \\ 4q &= 2n - 2 \\ q &= \frac{2n - 2}{4} \\ q &= \frac{1}{2}n - \frac{1}{2} \end{align} $$ A critical point is reached when $q = \frac{1}{2}n - \frac{1}{2}$.
    Since the second derivative is positive, then the critical point represents a local minimum, this also means that the first derivative increases as $q$ increases. This means that as $q$ approaches the critical point from $0$, it decreases, thus $0$ is a maximum for $f(q)$. Similarly, as $q$ approaches $q = n - 1$ from $q = \frac{1}{2}n - \frac{1}{2}$, it increases thus a maximum is also reached when $q = n - 1$.
